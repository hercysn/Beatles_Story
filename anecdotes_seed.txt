## 1. “A much better place—Paris”

> “It’s basically the start of a journey to France… It’s a much better place, Paris.”

—Paul McCartney, discussing “The End of the End”

### What happened

During an interview about his 2007 song “The End of the End,” Paul was asked about its description of death as the beginning of a journey to “a much better place.” The interviewer jokingly asked whether he meant somewhere better than England.

Paul responded that it was the beginning of a journey to France, before identifying the “much better place” as Paris. A surviving transcript records the exchange, although the original interview should still be located for the strongest possible citation.

### What is the connection?

Paris had a special place in John and Paul’s shared history. In autumn 1961, John used money he had received for his twenty-first birthday to take a trip with Paul. They originally intended to travel through France to Spain, but they stopped in Paris and stayed there instead.

This creates a striking possible connection: while discussing the journey at the end of life, Paul spontaneously names the city associated with one of his most private early adventures with John.

### Why is it important?

The moment links three different ideas:

* death as the beginning of another journey;
* Paris as a “better place”;
* John and Paul’s youthful journey to Paris.

It is emotionally suggestive, but the interpretation must remain carefully framed. Paul did **not** explicitly say that Paris represented John or that “The End of the End” was about John.

The safest formulation is:

> Paul’s reference to Paris may resonate with the journey he and John took there in 1961, although he did not explicitly make that connection.

### Evidence level

**Documented:** Paul called Paris “a much better place.”
**Documented:** John and Paul travelled to Paris together in 1961.
**Interpretation:** Paul’s answer may have evoked that shared journey.

## 2. George gets an electric shock during a road trip with Paul

> “Suddenly, George jumps up. I said, ‘What? What’s up?’”

—Paul McCartney

### What happened

Before the Beatles became famous, Paul and George Harrison took hitchhiking trips together through Wales and southern England.

On one journey, they were offered a ride on an electric milk float. George sat near or on the vehicle’s large battery. The metal zipper on the back of his jeans made contact with the battery, giving him an electric shock.

Paul later remembered:

> “He had a big zip mark on his butt… It brings you together.”

Paul’s account indicates that George’s trousers did not literally catch fire. The zipper conducted electricity and left a zipper-shaped mark or burn.

### What is the connection?

The story reveals a part of Paul and George’s relationship that is often overshadowed by later Beatles tensions.

They were not simply bandmates assigned to play together. Before fame, they were teenage friends who travelled with little money, accepted lifts from strangers, stayed in inexpensive lodgings and experienced ridiculous mishaps together.

Paul’s closing comment—“It brings you together”—turns the story from a funny accident into a reflection on friendship.

### Why is it important?

The anecdote establishes that Paul and George had their own independent bond before the Beatles.

George is frequently positioned only as the younger guitarist caught between Lennon and McCartney. This story instead shows him as Paul’s chosen travelling companion and fellow adventurer.

It also introduces the theme of memory. Paul said that Olivia Harrison had remembered a version in which the accident happened to Paul rather than George. The disagreement demonstrates how personal stories can change as they are retold—even among people close to the participants.

### Evidence level

**Documented recollection:** Paul and George went hitchhiking together.
**Paul’s later account:** George was shocked when his zipper contacted the battery.
**Uncertain detail:** Other people remembered the victim differently.

## 3. John spends his twenty-first-birthday money travelling with Paul

> “You got £100 off one of your rich relatives… so we decided we’d go to Spain.”

—Paul McCartney recalling the trip with John

### What happened

Ahead of his twenty-first birthday on October 9, 1961, John received £100 from relatives in Scotland. At the time, this was enough to finance a substantial holiday.

John and Paul left Liverpool on September 30. Their original plan was to travel through France and continue to Spain, but they stopped in Paris and remained there through John’s birthday.

Paul later recalled their improvised approach:

> “We decided we’d go to Spain. So we hitch-hiked…”

John reportedly told Aunt Mimi that he was travelling abroad to sell his paintings—an explanation that made the trip sound more practical than it really was.

### What is the connection?

John could have used the birthday money for records, clothes, instruments or a trip with someone else. Instead, he chose Paul as his travelling companion.

For a brief period, they stepped outside the usual Liverpool world of girlfriends, family, work and band obligations. They were simply two young men travelling through Europe together.

While in Paris, they also reconnected with their Hamburg friend Jürgen Vollmer. Vollmer’s brushed-forward hairstyle helped inspire the hairstyle that John and Paul adopted shortly afterward, contributing to the emerging visual identity of the Beatles.

### Why is it important?

The trip captures John and Paul at a transitional point:

* no longer schoolboys, but not yet famous;
* close musical partners, but still forming their identities;
* travelling as a pair shortly before the Beatles’ rise began.

It is significant not because it proves any particular theory about their relationship, but because John’s choice reveals whom he wanted beside him for an important personal milestone.

The trip also connects their private relationship with Beatles history. A birthday holiday became part of the development of the image they would soon present to the world.

### Evidence level

**Documented:** John received £100 and travelled with Paul.
**Documented:** They intended to reach Spain but stayed in Paris.
**Documented through later recollection:** The trip influenced their hairstyles.
**Interpretation:** John’s choice of Paul reflects the importance of their bond.

## 4. Paul’s “Coming Up” helps reignite John’s desire to record

> “It’s a good piece of work.”

—John Lennon on “Coming Up,” 1980

### What happened

Paul released “Coming Up” in 1980. The studio version was unusual and experimental: Paul created it largely by himself, manipulating the recording and altering the sound of his voice.

John heard the song during the final year of his life and responded positively. In his 1980 *Playboy* interview, the interviewer treated “Coming Up” as the notable exception when asking John about Paul’s post-Beatles music. John called it “a good piece of work.”

Paul later said:

> “Apparently it was the one song that got John recording again.”

He imagined John’s competitive reaction as:

> “Uh oh, I had better get working, too.”

This is Paul’s later understanding of the story, not a recorded statement from John that the song was his sole reason for returning.

### What is the connection?

During the Beatles, John and Paul constantly stimulated each other. When one wrote something strong or unexpected, the other felt challenged to respond.

“Coming Up” suggests that this creative relationship continued after the breakup. They no longer needed to sit in the same room or write “eyeball to eyeball.” John could hear Paul on the radio and immediately re-enter the old pattern of admiration, competition and artistic motivation.

Paul was still capable of surprising John—and John was still listening.

### Why is it important?

This may represent one of the final exchanges in the Lennon–McCartney musical conversation:

1. Paul makes an adventurous new record.
2. John hears and admires it.
3. The song contributes to John’s renewed creative excitement.
4. John returns to sustained recording and makes *Double Fantasy*.

It would be too strong to claim that “Coming Up” single-handedly caused John’s return. John had already been developing ideas, and his return involved Yoko Ono, new material and changes in his family life.

The more accurate conclusion is that Paul’s song served as **one meaningful creative spark**.

### Evidence level

**Direct contemporary quote:** John called “Coming Up” a good piece of work.
**Later recollection:** Paul heard that the song encouraged John to record again.
**Interpretation:** It continued their lifelong pattern of creative competition.

## 5. A blue light appears while Paul talks about John

> “That was John.”

—Paul McCartney, reportedly reacting to a blue light during an interview

### What happened

During footage associated with the 1995 *Beatles Anthology* period, Paul was speaking about John when a blue flash or flame-like light appeared in or passed through the shot.

Paul noticed it and jokingly identified it as John. In circulating versions of the clip, he reportedly asked that the moment remain in the finished footage.

The visual effect has sometimes been described online as an unexplained “blue flame.” However, no evidence establishes that it was supernatural. It may have been lens flare, reflected light, a camera artifact or another production effect.

At present, most easily accessible copies are reposted clips and fan uploads rather than a well-catalogued primary recording.

### What is the connection?

What matters historically is not whether the light had a mysterious cause. What matters is Paul’s instinctive reaction.

Rather than dismissing the interruption, he transformed it into a sign of John’s presence: “That was John.”

The response fits a broader pattern in Paul’s later life. He frequently describes remembering John as an active internal voice—imagining what John would say about a lyric, a decision or a piece of music.

### Why is it important?

The moment demonstrates how Paul keeps John present through humor and imagination.

Paul does not need to make a literal supernatural claim. His joke allows John to momentarily re-enter the room and the conversation. It reflects a continuing relationship maintained through memory.

For the website, this story should be categorized as a **symbolic moment**, not evidence of a paranormal event.

A careful presentation would say:

> When a blue light appeared as Paul discussed John, Paul playfully identified it as John’s presence. The source of the light is unknown, and Paul’s remark appears humorous rather than a formal supernatural claim.

### Evidence level

**Visible in circulating footage:** A blue light appears during the interview.
**Reported quote:** Paul identifies it as John.
**Unknown:** The original physical or optical source of the light.
**Fan interpretation:** The light was John’s spirit.

## 6. John meets Paul on July 6, 1957

> “I think what impressed him most was that I knew all the words.”

—Paul McCartney

### What happened

On July 6, 1957, fifteen-year-old Paul attended the Woolton Parish Church fête in Liverpool. His school friend Ivan Vaughan knew John and brought Paul to see John’s group, the Quarry Men.

John, then sixteen, performed with the group during the fête. Afterward, Ivan introduced him to Paul in the church-hall area.

Paul borrowed a guitar and performed Eddie Cochran’s “Twenty Flight Rock.” He also demonstrated that he knew how to tune a guitar and could remember complete lyrics—skills that impressed John.

Paul later reflected:

> “If I hadn’t known the chords… I might have got the elbow.”

John faced a decision. Paul was clearly talented, but bringing him into the Quarry Men meant admitting someone who might challenge John’s position as leader.

John later described his hesitation:

> “I’d been kingpin up to then.”

He ultimately decided that making the group stronger was worth sharing some control.

### What is the connection?

This is not only the day two future Beatles met. It immediately establishes the central dynamic of their relationship.

Paul impressed John, but also threatened him. John wanted Paul’s talent, even though he understood that Paul might become his equal.

Their partnership was therefore built from the beginning on two simultaneous forces:

* attraction to each other’s ability;
* competition over leadership, skill and recognition.

That combination would power their songwriting for years.

### Why is it important?

Almost every later story begins here:

* Paul joins John’s group.
* Paul introduces George.
* John and Paul begin writing songs together.
* Their partnership becomes the creative center of the Beatles.
* Their mixture of affection and rivalry reshapes popular music.

The modest setting makes the event especially powerful. One of the most consequential artistic partnerships of the twentieth century began at a local church fête between two teenagers.

July 6 can therefore function as the website’s foundational anniversary: not yet the birth of the Beatles, but the beginning of the relationship that made the Beatles possible.

### Evidence level

**Well documented:** John and Paul were introduced at the Woolton fête on July 6, 1957.
**Later recollection:** Paul performed “Twenty Flight Rock” and impressed John.
**John’s later recollection:** He worried that Paul could challenge his leadership.
